# PYRAXIS — MASTER MOTION BIBLE

**Role of this document:** permanent reference for experience design. Any future AI or developer building an animation, transition, or interaction for PYRAXIS reads this first. If a proposed change isn't traceable to a rule in here, it doesn't ship until this document is updated to account for it.

**Governing idea, stated once, referenced forever:** PYRAXIS is not a website with sections. It is one visible system — a machine made of light and particles that the visitor scrolls *through*, not *past*. Every section is the same system wearing a different configuration. Nothing new is ever introduced; things that were already there simply reorganize into what you need to see next.

---

# PART A — THE EXPERIENCE

## 1. Experience Story

The emotional arc of the site is a single sentence: **doubt → clarity → proof → decision → quiet confidence.** Ten sections, one arc, no detours.

### Hero
**Feel:** arrival at something already running. Not "welcome," more "you've just walked into a room where a machine has been working the whole time."
**Visual transformation:** the PYRAXIS mark is not typed or faded in — it is *assembled*, particles converging from the dark into the wordmark, holding for a breath, then relaxing into ambient drift. The assembly proves the system is real before a single word of copy is read.
**Particles:** dense, purposeful, converging with visible intent (fast approach, decisive settle) — this is the only moment in the site particles move with urgency. Every later moment is calmer by comparison, which is what makes this one read as an "opening."
**Lighting:** near-total darkness (`#020205`) with the mark as the only light source in the frame — everything the visitor's eye can do is look at the one lit thing.
**Motion communicates:** *this already exists and works.* Confidence, not a pitch.

### Hero → Problem
**Feel:** the confidence cracks, deliberately. The visitor is shown why they're here.
**Visual transformation:** the assembled mark doesn't fade — it *disperses*, the same particles that built it scattering outward and losing their purple accent-glow, becoming plain dust. The system that was whole a moment ago is now shown fragmented.
**Particles:** transition from `signal` (purple, focused) to `dust` (silver, scattered) — a literal color-coded emotional state change.
**Lighting:** glow contracts, ambient light dims further — the room gets a little colder.
**Motion communicates:** *this is what happens without a system.*

### Problem
**Feel:** recognition. "Yes, that's my business." Slight discomfort, not alarm.
**Visual transformation:** scattered particles loosely orbit a small set of problem icons — missed calls, lost leads, manual busywork — never fully still, always slightly restless, communicating friction rather than crisis.
**Particles:** low density, erratic micro-movement (the wobble noise function at higher amplitude than anywhere else in the site) — this is the one place particles are allowed to look *unresolved*.
**Lighting:** flat, slightly desaturated. No glow anywhere in this section — glow is earned later, not given here.
**Motion communicates:** friction, entropy, cost of doing nothing.

### Problem → Growth System
**Feel:** relief arriving. The turn.
**Visual transformation:** the restless dust particles stop wobbling and begin, one by one, to accelerate toward a shared center — the same particles, newly purposeful. This is the site's first "aha," and it is entirely a particle-behavior change, not a new element appearing.
**Particles:** wobble noise drops to near-zero, drag/pull constants sharpen — particles visibly decide to cooperate.
**Lighting:** first appearance of the purple accent glow since the Hero — signals "the system is switching back on."
**Motion communicates:** *order is available.*

### Growth System
**Feel:** understanding. "Oh — it's a loop." Satisfaction of a mechanism clicking into place.
**Visual transformation:** particles resolve into the seven-node cycle — **Attract → Capture → Convert → Nurture → Review → Retain → Analyze** — arranged in a literal circle, connected by thin signal lines that pulse in sequence around the ring rather than firing all at once. The visitor should be able to *watch the loop run* for a few seconds before scrolling on.
**Particles:** now behaving as a closed system — conservation of motion, no particle leaves the ring's gravity without a scroll-triggered reason.
**Lighting:** the connecting lines are the first true "energy" cue in the site — thin, animated, directional. This is the visual grammar for "information flowing" that recurs through Portfolio and Process.
**Motion communicates:** *this isn't a pitch deck, it's a mechanism, and mechanisms don't lie.*

### Growth System → Infrastructure (Growth Engines)
**Feel:** zooming out from *what it does* to *what it's built on*.
**Visual transformation:** the seven-node ring doesn't disappear — it contracts and recedes into the background, becoming a faint structural glow behind the five infrastructure engines (AI & Automation, Scalable Infrastructure, Seamless Integrations, Modular & Flexible, Secure by Design) that assemble in front of it. The loop is now literally the foundation the engines stand on — visible depth, not a cut.
**Particles:** split into five discrete streams, one per engine, each stream a slightly different density/speed signature (AI's stream flickers faster — "thinking"; Infrastructure's stream is steadiest — "load-bearing").
**Lighting:** individual glow per engine card on hover only — at rest, uniform, quiet. Hover is where this section earns its "alive" moment.
**Motion communicates:** *the loop works because of what's underneath it.*

### Infrastructure → Why PYRAXIS
**Feel:** the pitch softens into a promise. Less mechanism, more reassurance.
**Visual transformation:** the five engine streams draw together into a single calmer field, density lowers, particle size increases slightly (bigger, slower, fewer — the visual signature of "settled" rather than "working").
**Particles:** lowest velocity in the site outside the Footer.
**Lighting:** warmest, most even light in the entire experience — this section should feel like the exhale after the mechanism was proven.
**Motion communicates:** *you can trust this now — here's why.*

### Why PYRAXIS → Portfolio
**Feel:** "okay, prove it with receipts."
**Visual transformation:** the calm field begins drifting horizontally rather than vertically — the first directional change in the whole scroll — telegraphing that the interaction model is about to shift (Portfolio is a horizontal rail).
**Particles:** bend from vertical drift to horizontal drift over the transition's full duration, not instantly — this is a redirect (§Motion Language), never a cut.
**Lighting:** accent color per project card begins to assert itself here — proof carries its own color signature (each project keeps the shared purple, but card-level glow intensity varies with the stat being shown — bigger win, brighter glow).
**Motion communicates:** *specificity. Real outcomes, not a mood board.*

### Portfolio → Process
**Feel:** "how do I get one of those outcomes." Curiosity about mechanism resumes.
**Visual transformation:** horizontal drift resolves back to vertical as the rail ends — mirrors the Why PYRAXIS → Portfolio handoff exactly, so the visitor unconsciously recognizes "we're back to normal scrolling" without being told.
**Particles:** re-densify slightly and begin arranging into a left-to-right sequence — process stages forming like a queue.
**Lighting:** each process stage lights up in sequence as it's reached, previous stages dim (not disappear) — literal storytelling of "the previous step led here."
**Motion communicates:** *there is a repeatable procedure, not a black box.*

### Process → Future-Proof Systems
**Feel:** the horizon opens. From "how we work" to "how far this goes."
**Visual transformation:** the last process-stage's particles don't finish and stop — they continue moving, overshooting into the Future-Proof pillars, arriving as those pillars' entrance particles. The two sections share one particle event, split across a scroll boundary — this is the clearest instance of "nothing simply appears" in the whole site.
**Particles:** speed increases slightly for the first time since the Hero — this is the site's second "energy" beat, positioned deliberately before the CTA so momentum is already built when the ask arrives.
**Lighting:** glow begins to expand outward from pinpoint (engine cards, process stages) to field-wide — the light source is no longer a single object, it's becoming ambient, foreshadowing the CTA's globe.
**Motion communicates:** *this system scales with you, not just with today's problem.*

### Future-Proof Systems → CTA
**Feel:** the site's single emotional peak. Convergence, culmination, "this is the moment."
**Visual transformation:** every particle stream that has existed up to this point — Hero's dust, Problem's scatter, Growth System's ring, the engine streams, the process sequence — visually converges toward one point: the globe. This is the only `transformation`-tier (not `section`-tier) transition duration permitted outside the Hero, because it's earned the extra time.
**Particles:** maximum density and maximum directional agreement anywhere in the site — everything moving the same way, for the first time.
**Lighting:** brightest, most saturated purple in the entire experience, concentrated at the globe. Everything before this section was training the eye to associate brightness with "resolution" — this is the payoff.
**Motion communicates:** *arrival. This is what all of that was building toward.*

### CTA → Footer
**Feel:** the machine idling down. Calm, not abrupt.
**Visual transformation:** the globe's convergence relaxes — particles that were pulled tight loosen and drift outward, slowing, the way a spinning object coasts to rest rather than being switched off.
**Particles:** density recedes toward zero across the Footer's full height — a genuine ending, not a repeat of the Hero's density.
**Lighting:** returns to near-Hero darkness, but without the mark — the room is dark because the show is over, not because nothing has happened yet. Same darkness, opposite meaning, and the visitor should feel that difference.
**Motion communicates:** *confidence at rest.* The last feeling should be "that was solid," not "that's over."

## 2. Motion Story — One Unified Philosophy

Every animation in the site is a dialect of one idea: **information becoming energy becoming order.**

Concretely, every motion event in PYRAXIS is one of exactly four story-beats, and every future animation must be classified as one before it's built:

1. **Convergence** — scattered things gaining purpose (Hero assembly, Growth System ring forming, Future-Proof → CTA collapse). Reads as *understanding arriving*.
2. **Divergence** — ordered things releasing (Hero → Problem dispersal, CTA → Footer relaxation). Reads as *tension released*, always slower than its convergence counterpart.
3. **Circulation** — closed-loop motion with no net gain or loss (Growth System's ring, Process's sequence). Reads as *a working mechanism*, not a one-off effect.
4. **Transference** — motion that carries directly from one section into the next without resetting (Process → Future-Proof handoff). Reads as *continuity*, and is the rarest, most valuable beat — reserved for the handful of transitions that most need to prove "this is one system."

No animation is permitted that isn't one of these four. If a new feature's motion doesn't fit, the feature's *concept* is wrong for this site, not just its animation.

## 3. Particle Story — Lifecycle as Character

Particles are the site's only recurring character. They don't represent decoration; they represent **information moving through a system that is PYRAXIS itself.** Design better than a literal "particles become X" checklist — give them a consistent internal logic instead:

**The rule particles always obey:** a particle is either *dust* (ambient, unclaimed information — silver) or *signal* (claimed, purposeful information — purple). A particle's entire visible lifecycle is the story of dust becoming signal, and, eventually, signal returning to dust. That transformation, not the shape it momentarily holds, is the character arc.

**Lifecycle across the site:**
- **Born** in the Hero as signal — the system's origin state is order, not chaos, which is why the site opens with assembly, not scatter.
- **Demoted** to dust at Hero → Problem — the story's inciting incident is literally particles losing their purpose.
- **Restless** through Problem — dust particles orbiting loosely, unclaimed, describing the cost of no system.
- **Reclaimed** at Problem → Growth System — dust re-earns signal status one particle at a time (not all at once — the recovery is visibly gradual, which is more convincing than an instant fix).
- **Circulating** through Growth System — signal particles now conserved in a closed loop, the site's proof-of-concept.
- **Specializing** through Infrastructure — signal splits into five distinguishable streams, each with its own personality (speed, flicker, steadiness) matching its engine's meaning.
- **Softening** through Why PYRAXIS — signal particles slow and grow, becoming reassuring rather than industrious.
- **Bearing witness** through Portfolio — signal particles take on each project's accent intensity, becoming the visual proof of outcomes rather than process.
- **Sequencing** through Process — signal particles form a legible left-to-right queue, demonstrating repeatability.
- **Accelerating** through Future-Proof — signal gains speed and starts pulling toward one point, building anticipation.
- **Converging** at CTA — every signal particle in play visually arrives at the globe simultaneously. This is the only moment the entire particle population acts as one body.
- **Released** at Footer — signal particles are demoted back to dust, gently, and allowed to drift to stillness. The story ends where dust began, but the visitor has watched it mean something now.

This is a complete circle, not a one-way journey — dust → signal → dust — which is a better structural echo of PYRAXIS's own pitch (turning unclaimed opportunity into working system) than a straight line would be.

## 4. Lighting Story

Lighting in PYRAXIS is not decorative — it is the site's honesty meter. **Brightness is earned, never given.** A section is only as lit as it has proven itself.

- **Hero:** single light source (the mark). Everything else dark. Establishes: light = the system, darkness = the absence of it.
- **Problem:** no glow anywhere. The section is deliberately the least attractive-looking in the site — discomfort should look like discomfort.
- **Growth System:** first return of glow, but localized — only the connecting lines between nodes carry light, nothing else. Light follows function, not decoration.
- **Infrastructure:** glow is conditional (hover-only at rest) — the visitor has to *engage* to be shown light, mirroring "the system works, but you have to use it."
- **Why PYRAXIS:** the warmest, most ambient, least directional lighting in the site — the one section allowed to feel comfortable rather than earned, because it's the emotional rest stop before the proof section.
- **Portfolio:** light becomes proof-specific — each card's glow intensity is tied to its actual result, so brightness literally correlates to outcome size across this section.
- **Process:** sequential lighting — only the active stage is lit, previous stages dim to a low ambient hold. Light as narrator, showing you where you are in a sequence.
- **Future-Proof:** glow expands from pinpoint to field — the site's first hint that light is about to stop being local and start being everywhere.
- **CTA:** peak brightness and peak saturation, concentrated at the globe — the site's single boldest lighting choice, and it only works because everything before it was quiet.
- **Footer:** return to near-total darkness — not a regression, an ending. The story closes on the same visual value it opened on, which is the whole point.

**Rule for all future sections:** before adding glow, bloom, or highlight to a new element, ask "has this element earned brightness yet, narratively?" If the answer requires justification, it's too early.

## 5. Camera Story

There is no literal camera (no WebGL scene) outside the CTA globe today — this section governs the *illusion* of one, produced entirely through parallax depth, tilt, and particle-field behavior. It must never announce itself; a visitor should feel more immersed without being able to say why.

- **Mouse movement:** governs a near-imperceptible depth parallax (background/midground/foreground layers moving at different speeds) plus a 1–2° maximum tilt on hero-weight objects. This is the camera "breathing," not moving — it should read as the site *noticing* the visitor's presence, nothing more assertive than that.
- **Scroll movement:** the primary camera driver. Scroll position maps to depth and focus changes continuously — never a hard section-swap. Scroll velocity affects how eagerly the particle field responds (faster scroll = particles catch up more urgently), giving scroll itself a physical, weighted feel rather than a simple progress-bar mapping.
- **Depth:** communicated through particle-layer speed differential and slight luminance stepping (foreground marginally brighter), never through heavy blur — blur is reserved as a rare punctuation mark (see globe focus pull), not a constant atmosphere.
- **Zoom:** essentially absent except at the CTA convergence, where the visual sense of "pulling in" toward the globe is the site's one deliberate zoom-equivalent moment — earned by being rare.
- **Rotation:** slow enough to never be caught in the act — the globe (and any future 3D element) should look different after ten seconds of attention without the visitor ever seeing it move.
- **Perspective:** shallow, consistent (1000–1400px CSS perspective range) across every section that uses tilt — a visitor moving through the site should never feel the "lens" change, even though the content around it completely transforms.

**Governing rule:** the camera's job is to make the visitor feel present, not to perform. If a camera effect is describable as "cool," it's too strong.

## 6. Visual Language — One Universe

Every visual element, regardless of section, must read as belonging to the same object. The tells:

- **Cards** are always the same surface family (`flat`, `glass`, `metal`, `signal` — no fifth type invented). Portfolio cards and Infrastructure cards look like siblings, not cousins.
- **Icons** are line-based, single-weight, no fills except on active/hover state — icons are outlines waiting to be lit, matching the "brightness is earned" lighting rule.
- **Particles** are always one of exactly two types (dust/signal) — no section is allowed a bespoke particle color or shape. If Portfolio "needs" gold particles for its stats, the answer is no — use signal purple at higher intensity instead.
- **Typography** is two voices only: Cormorant Garamond (display, always large, always the "human" voice — headlines, promises) and Syne (sans, structural — labels, data, the "system" voice). Every section alternates between exactly these two, never a third.
- **Glow** always uses the same falloff curve and the same purple value family (`#8b5cf6`/`#a78bfa`/`#7c3aed`) — a glow in Footer must be mathematically the same treatment as a glow in Hero, just at different intensity.
- **Borders** are single-pixel, low-contrast (`#1a1a2e`), used only on `glass` surfaces — never decorative, always structural (defining where an interactive surface ends).
- **Spacing** follows one vertical rhythm across all sections — section padding and internal gaps should be visually derivable from the same base unit, so a visitor's eye doesn't have to recalibrate between sections.
- **Connections** (the thin lines linking nodes/cards/data points) are the site's signature graphic device — introduced in Growth System, they are allowed to reappear anywhere information relationships need showing (Infrastructure, Process, Future-Proof), but never as pure decoration — a connection line must always represent a real relationship in the content.

**The single test for whether something belongs:** could this element be moved into any other section of the site without looking imported? If not, it isn't finished.

# PART B — THE BIBLE (Binding Rules)

## Motion Philosophy
Motion is infrastructure made visible. Nothing appears — everything transforms from something that was already there. See Part A §1–6 for the full narrative reasoning; this Part codifies it into enforceable rules.

## Motion Language
Six permitted verbs, no others: **Arrive, Settle, Drift, Redirect, Recede, Hold.** Four permitted story-beats, no others: **Convergence, Divergence, Circulation, Transference** (§2). Every animation must be describable using only this vocabulary.

## Emotional Goals
Confidence over excitement. Depth over spectacle. Continuity over novelty. The visitor should feel *reassured*, never *entertained* — entertainment is a competitor's strategy, not this brand's.

## Animation Hierarchy
- **Primary** (section-defining): one at a time per viewport, never competing with a second primary motion.
- **Secondary** (supporting, e.g. card stagger): always subordinate in timing to a primary trigger — never fires independently.
- **Ambient** (particle drift, glow pulse): must never draw the eye away from primary/secondary content.

## Easing Curves
| Purpose | Curve |
|---|---|
| Entrance | `power3.out` / `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` |
| Exit / recede | `power2.out` / `cubic-bezier(0.25, 1, 0.5, 1)` |
| Hover / micro | `power2.out` / `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` |
| Scroll-linked | `power1.inOut` / `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.6, 1)` |
| Ambient/continuous | sine-based custom function, not a GSAP ease |
| Section transition | `power2.inOut` / `cubic-bezier(0.65, 0, 0.35, 1)` |

No elastic/bounce/spring overshoot anywhere in the primary content layer. The only physics-based exception is the particle system's own mass/drag simulation (never a GSAP spring ease).

## Timing System
| Tier | Range | Use |
|---|---|---|
| `micro` | 120–250ms | cursor, focus, icon swap |
| `hover` | 250–400ms | card lift, button, link |
| `section` | 800–1400ms | headline reveal, stagger group |
| `transformation` | 1500–3000ms | section transition, Hero assembly, CTA convergence |

Only two moments in the entire site are permitted to use the full `transformation` ceiling with a story-justified extension: Hero's opening assembly, and Future-Proof → CTA convergence. Every other transition stays within tier.

## Particle Rules
Two types only: `dust` (silver, ambient/unclaimed) and `signal` (purple, purposeful/claimed) — see Part A §3 for the full lifecycle logic this encodes. Density budget by device tier: desktop 3000–8000, tablet 1200–2500, mobile 400–1200, scaled further by live fps (`degradeFactor` 0.4–1.0) and halved under reduced motion. Speed capped, never teleports, cursor-proximity repulsion only (no attraction-to-cursor). One canvas, one pool, one render loop for the entire site — no section owns its own particle system.

## Lighting Rules
Brightness must be narratively earned per Part A §4 — no section receives glow it hasn't justified. Glow is reserved for `signal` particles and active/interactive states; a resting `flat` surface never glows. Purple visual weight (particles + surfaces combined) capped at 5% of any frame. No global bloom, ever — bloom is reserved exclusively for the CTA convergence moment.

## Camera Rules
Mouse-driven tilt capped at 1–2°. Perspective held constant at 1000–1400px across every section that uses it. Depth communicated via layer speed differential and slight luminance stepping, not blur. Zoom effectively reserved for the CTA moment only. See Part A §5.

## Interaction Rules
Buttons/cards/links use `hover` tier, `power2.out`, max 1.02–1.03x scale. Every interactive element needs a visible focus state distinct from hover. Touch equivalents required for every hover-dependent effect. Active/press states always *reduce* scale (never increase) — press reads as "pushed in."

## Scroll Rules
One global scroll listener (`ScrollProvider`), all sections read from it — no section attaches its own. Section transitions are always scroll-linked/scrubbed, never autoplay-on-arrival. Scroll velocity affects particle-field urgency (§5) but never bypasses the timing-tier ceiling.

## Section Transition Rules
Every boundary in Part A §1 is implemented as a particle-field reconfiguration first, a bespoke visual effect only if the particle system genuinely cannot carry the moment (currently true only for the CTA globe). No boundary may be a hard cut once particle consolidation is complete — an interim clean cross-fade is acceptable only until then.

## Performance Budget
Target 60fps desktop/tablet, degrade trigger at 45fps, unacceptable floor 30fps. Max particles per device tier as above. One canvas, one draw call pass per frame, site-wide. No independent per-section canvases, resize listeners, or mousemove listeners — one of each, globally, for the whole application.

## Accessibility
`prefers-reduced-motion` halves ambient density and disables mouse-repulsion forces (already correct — preserve exactly). Global CSS fallback collapses remaining transitions. Every interactive element keyboard-reachable with a visible, non-suppressed focus state. Contrast AA minimum on all text-on-surface pairs. No flashing/pulsing above 3Hz anywhere.

## Desktop Behavior
Full system: parallax, tilt, cursor, max density, all transitions active, fine-pointer-only effects enabled.

## Mobile Behavior
Mobile-floor particle density, no cursor, no mouse-parallax, tap-equivalent for every hover state, section transitions simplified toward straightforward reveal-on-scroll rather than elaborate cross-fade — the mobile experience should feel intentionally quieter, not like a degraded desktop.

## Reduced Motion Strategy
Never a blanket "kill all motion" — density and force reduction, not elimination, so the particle field stays legible as content rather than becoming inert. Governed centrally by `AnimationProvider`, queried, never re-detected per-component.

## WebGL Guidelines
Canvas2D is sufficient for every particle/dust/signal system, icon canvas, cursor, and ambient effect currently in the site — do not port these to WebGL. The single sanctioned WebGL use case is the CTA/Future-Proof globe (true sphere, rotation, depth, light falloff) — the one place 2D physically cannot produce the needed result. No second WebGL scene without a documented forcing function added to this bible first.

## Canvas2D Guidelines
One canvas, one particle pool, one render loop, for the entire application (post-consolidation) — every particle-driven visual across every section sends instructions to this single engine rather than owning independent canvas/rAF/resize infrastructure.

## Shader Guidelines
Reserved, optional, lowest priority: a restrained GLSL noise field could eventually replace part of the Hero's ambient layer for organic cheap-on-GPU motion. Not required for the experience described in Part A to work — evaluate only after the globe ships and only if performance budget allows.

## Design Rules
Every new animation must: declare its timing tier and pull duration/easing from the shared tokens; have a true rest state unless explicitly ambient; decelerate on entrance and exit (exit slower/quieter); degrade under reduced-motion and performance tier; read from shared global input sources; be classifiable as one of the four story-beats (§2); be traceable to a reason in the experience story (Part A §1) before it ships.

## Things Never Allowed
Elastic/bounce/spring overshoot. New global event listeners outside the existing provider layer. New independent canvases outside the consolidated engine. Global bloom or global blur. Purple exceeding its 5% weight cap. Loading spinners or skeleton shimmer. Idle looping animation on primary content at rest. Motion that can't be reduced under `prefers-reduced-motion`. A section transition that is a hard cut with no particle continuity, once consolidation has shipped. Any new particle color, shape, timing tier, or easing curve introduced without updating this document in the same change.

# PART C — EXPERIENCE ROADMAP

The order below is chosen because each milestone is a **precondition** for the one after it — building lighting or the globe before the underlying plumbing exists would mean redoing the plumbing work twice.

**Milestone 1 — Interaction Unification**
Consolidate the four competing mousemove listeners into `MouseProvider`'s single external store. *Why first:* every later milestone (camera, particles, lighting-on-hover) reads mouse position — building on four disagreeing sources guarantees rework later.

**Milestone 2 — Particle Continuity**
Migrate the nine independent canvases into the single global `ParticleEngine`. *Why second:* this is the literal mechanism for Part A's entire particle story (§3) — none of the described transformations (Hero assembly → dispersal → Growth System ring → etc.) are physically possible across section boundaries until this exists. Everything in Part A is aspirational until this milestone ships.

**Milestone 3 — Motion Language**
Backfill every GSAP call to pull duration/easing from the shared token system (§Timing/Easing in Part B), and classify every existing animation into one of the four story-beats. *Why third:* with one particle system now in place, this is where the site's individual animations stop being independently-tuned and start speaking one dialect — cheap to do now, expensive to retrofit after more animations are added on top of the old pattern.

**Milestone 4 — Section Transition System**
Build out the nine transition specifications from Part A §1 (Problem→Growth, Growth→Infrastructure, etc.) as particle-instruction configurations. *Why fourth:* requires both Milestone 2 (single particle field to configure) and Milestone 3 (consistent timing/easing to drive it) — attempting this earlier would mean hand-building bespoke effects per boundary, exactly the "collection of sections" problem this whole document exists to solve.

**Milestone 5 — Lighting System**
Implement the earned-brightness progression from Part A §4 across all ten sections. *Why fifth:* lighting changes are cheap to apply once the particle/motion plumbing is unified (glow is fundamentally a particle-engine and CSS property), but meaningless to design in isolation before the sections actually transition into each other.

**Milestone 6 — WebGL Globe**
Build the CTA/Future-Proof globe scene in true 3D. *Why sixth, not earlier:* it's the site's single biggest new-dependency risk (introduces `three` for the first time) and its payoff (Part A's convergence moment) only lands if everything leading up to it — particle continuity, motion language, transitions, lighting — is already making the visitor feel the buildup. A great globe on top of disconnected sections is wasted work.

**Milestone 7 — Polish**
Cross-boundary QA pass (real browser, real devices, real Lighthouse/FPS traces — none exist yet per the technical audit), accessibility sweep, responsive behavior tuning per device tier, final performance-budget verification against Part B's numbers. *Why last:* polish on an unfinished system just polishes the wrong thing twice.

**Explicit non-goals for this roadmap:** no milestone here is a redesign or a rewrite. Every milestone operates on infrastructure and configuration that already exists in the codebase in partial form — the roadmap's job is finishing and connecting what's already been built, not replacing it.

---

*This document is the single source of truth for PYRAXIS motion and experience design. Part A explains why; Part B enforces how; Part C sequences when. Update all three together — a change to one without the others is how a site stops being one system and goes back to being a collection of sections.*
